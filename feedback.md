Okay, I've now seen the full app — home, upload modal, review page, history, and chat panel, plus the frontend stack (React 19, shadcn, Tailwind, Vite). Here's my honest UI/UX assessment.

The good — and it is genuinely good:
The dark theme is well-executed. You're not just using "dark background + white text" — the card layering with subtle border gradients gives it depth. The teal/cyan accent color is consistent and works well against the dark palette. The confidence badges (green High, yellow Medium, red Low) are immediately intuitive — no learning curve. The "related:" tags linking action items to risks and other items are a standout feature. I haven't seen that in Fireflies, Otter, or any of the major meeting tools. It's the kind of detail that makes someone think "this understands my meeting, not just my words."
The upload modal is clean — file drop plus paste text is the right dual approach. The three feature cards at the bottom (Decisions, Action Items, Risks) are a nice onboarding touch that sets expectations before the user even submits.
Now, the honest critique — what I'd change with unlimited time:
1. The review page is a vertical scroll of doom. You have 7 decisions + 13 action items + 3 risks all stacked vertically in cards. That's easily 15-20 screen heights of scrolling. For a 9-minute meeting. Imagine a 60-minute meeting — it becomes unusable. The core problem is that every item gets the same visual weight. D1 (proposal approved — high confidence, clear decision) looks identical to D4 (attendance count — arguably not even a decision). I'd rethink this with a summary-first, drill-down-second approach. Show a compact table or list view by default — one line per item with ID, description, confidence badge, and owner. Let users click into any item to expand the full card with source quote, rationale, and related items. Think of it like an email inbox: you scan the list, then open what matters.
2. The "Made by: empty" across every decision is a visual credibility killer. Even if the data isn't available, showing the word "empty" in italics is worse than just hiding the field. If made_by is null, don't render that row at all. Same for "Deadline: empty" on action items. Empty fields make the AI look broken. A simple rule: if the value is null or empty, hide the field entirely; if you want to signal incompleteness, use a subtle "attribution not detected" in muted text.
3. The meeting cards on the home page both say "pasted-transcript.txt". That's the filename, not the meeting. After analysis, you already have the inferred title ("Hawaii Demo and Collaboration Opportunities"). Use that as the card title, with the filename as secondary metadata. Right now if someone has 20 meetings, they'd see 20 cards all saying "pasted-transcript.txt" or "meeting.vtt" with no way to distinguish them at a glance.
4. The nav structure is underusing the space. You have Home, Live, History, and a provider dropdown. But the real workflow is: upload → analyze → review → approve → ask questions about past meetings. The nav doesn't reflect this flow. I'd consider a sidebar layout for the review page specifically — transcript on the left, analysis on the right (or vice versa). This way users can cross-reference what the AI extracted against what was actually said. Right now the transcript disappears entirely after upload — you have to trust the AI blindly.
5. The chat panel feels like an afterthought. It slides in as a narrow right panel that overlaps the main content. For something as powerful as "ask anything about your meetings," it deserves better real estate. I'd either make it a full page (like a dedicated "Ask" route) or make it a proper split-pane that doesn't overlay the content. Also, the chat has no conversation history visible — just "Ask anything about your meetings" and an input. Adding 2-3 suggested prompts ("What action items are overdue?", "Summarize decisions from last week", "Who has the most open tasks?") would dramatically increase engagement.
6. The Trust Flags section is at the very top of the review page, which is great positioning, but it doesn't link to the problematic items. When it says "8 source quote(s) could not be verified," the user has no way to know which 8 without scrolling through every single item. I'd either mark unverified items with a small warning icon on their card, or make the trust flag clickable so it filters/highlights the affected items.
7. There's no way to edit or reject individual items in the review. The only action is "Approve & Export" for the whole thing. For a "human-in-the-loop" tool, this is a significant gap. Users should be able to delete a bad decision (like D3/D4 duplicates), edit a description, reassign an owner, or mark an item as "not actually a decision." Without this, the review step is read-only — it's "take it or leave it," not "review and refine." I noticed the "Verified" badges on action items, which suggests some of this exists, but I couldn't find a way to remove or edit items.
8. Color and visual hierarchy could be stronger. Right now every card has the same dark background with a subtle teal border. The sections (Decisions, Action Items, Risks) are differentiated only by their heading text and a small colored icon. I'd give each section a distinct but subtle accent — maybe a faint green tint for decisions, blue for action items, orange/red for risks. Just enough to create visual landmarks so you can tell where you are while scrolling.
9. The History page is bare. It's just a flat list of two items. As this scales, you'd need search, filters (by status, date range, participant), and probably a way to compare meetings or track action items across meetings. The "Knowledge Base" button I saw on the review page suggests you're thinking about cross-meeting intelligence — the History page should be the gateway to that.

I didn't go through the full analysis flow, but given that you have a 3-pass pipeline hitting an LLM multiple times, the analysis probably takes 15-30 seconds. Users need to see what's happening — "Extracting entities... Building relationships... Synthesizing report..." with a progress indicator. A blank loading spinner for 30 seconds will make people think it's broken.
